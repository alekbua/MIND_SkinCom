# MIND_SkinCom
Codes associated with manuscript, "Synthetic Skin Microbial Community (SkinCom) Models the Functional Prioritization of the Human Face Microbiome"

## Abstract

Advances in metagenomic sequencing (metaG) have established the foundation of our understanding of the skin microbiome. However, metaG alone is insufficient for generating the mechanistic insights that offer deep biological understanding. To this end, we developed and validated a multi-omics analysis combining metatranslatomic (metaRS) with metaG information called Microbial Interaction and Niche Determination (MIND), allowing us to design microbial interventions with predictable outcomes.

Here, we apply MIND to both a synthetic skin microbial community (SkinCom) and the native human microbiome to reveal resource allocation, pathway prioritization, substrate preferences, and competition. We show that Cutibacterium acnes dominates the human face microbiome by composition, though metaRS profiles reveal more distributed activity across multiple genera. Our SkinCom effectively recapitulates the native microbiome, with a strong correlation between their observed metabolic expression (R2 = 0.83), and with 16 out of 20 top pathways agreeing between SkinCom and face swab datasets. The largest discrepancy was due to our limited growth medium not containing every compound that the cells actively consumed in the rich skin environment, including a number of amino acids.. Regardless, we observed consistent interspecies competition prediction (59.72% agreement). Overall, we thus demonstrate that MIND reveals resource allocation, substrate utilization, and competition dynamics. Further utilizing this information, MIND aids the discovery of novel microbiome editing targets, such as niche-specific substrates (prebiotics) and competition-informed bacterial addition (probiotics).
